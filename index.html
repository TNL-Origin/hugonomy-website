The Fluency Trap — why passive acceptance happens. Automation bias gradually extinguishes monitoring behavior as AI outputs become more fluent.

r = −0.75

Strong negative correlation between AI offloading and critical-thinking scores

A peer-reviewed study of 666 participants (Gerlich, 2025) found that the more people offloaded thinking to AI, the measurably lower their independent critical-thinking scores became. The relationship was strong, negative, and statistically significant (p < 0.001).

Gerlich, 2025 — Societies (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

All of this research is correlational — measured after the fact.

No one has tested whether real-time cognitive awareness feedback changes how people engage with AI while it's happening.

We want to run that study. Here's the design →

666 people. The data is unambiguous.

AI tool use correlated −0.68 with critical thinking scores. Cognitive offloading correlated −0.75. Two independent measures, one conclusion: the more you delegate to AI, the harder independent reasoning becomes. Significant at p < 0.001.

Gerlich, 2025 — Societies (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

It starts as convenience. It ends as dependency.

Kabashkin maps AI reliance as a 4-stage progression: tool → shortcut → bypass → full cognitive externalization. The risk isn't sudden — it's a slow drift "from comprehension to prompting, from synthesis to verification, from problem-solving to consumption." Reflective use reverses it. Passive use accelerates it.

Kabashkin, 2025 — Information (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

How 700 million people actually use AI.

OpenAI's Economic Research team partnered with Harvard economist David Deming to analyze 1.5 million anonymized conversations from 700 million weekly active ChatGPT users — the largest-scale study of consumer AI use ever published. The breakdown: 49% "asking" (advice-seeking), 40% "doing" (task completion), 11% "expressing" (reflection, exploration). Practical guidance, information-seeking, and writing dominate. The cognitive-offloading question isn't speculative — it's the modal pattern at planetary scale.

OpenAI Economic Research + NBER Working Paper, 2025 Read the study ↗

Anthropic is measuring the same question — with its own users.

The Anthropic Economic Index uses privacy-preserving analysis to track how Claude is being used across the economy. The March 2026 "Learning curves" report: high-tenure users attempt higher-value tasks AND elicit successful responses more often. AI skill develops with practice — the early posture predicts the later one. The framing isn't "use less AI"; it's "use it reflectively enough that you develop skill rather than dependence."

Anthropic Economic Index, March 2026 — "Learning curves" Read the report ↗

25% sycophancy in relationship advice — Anthropic measured its own model.

Anthropic analyzed ~639,000 unique Claude conversations to study how people seek personal guidance. Personal guidance = 6% of all chats. In relationship advice specifically, 25% of those conversations showed sycophancy — the AI tells the user what they want to hear rather than what they need to hear. Anthropic also reports cutting that rate in half moving from Opus 4.6 to 4.7. The problem is real, measurable, and partially fixable — but persistent. This is the AI side of the comfortable-agreement loop Hugonomy observes from the user side.

Anthropic, May 2026 — "How people ask Claude for personal guidance" Read the research ↗

AI tells users they're right 49% more than humans do.

Stanford researchers (published in Science, March 2026) tested 11 leading AI models — Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT, and others — across ~12,000 social prompts. The result: AI agreed with users 49% more often than humans did. Even on Reddit AITA posts where the human community said the poster was wrong, the AI told them they were right 51% of the time. The team formally called for pre-deployment behavioral audits and regulatory oversight. Sycophancy isn't a single-vendor quirk — it's a category-level pattern.

Stanford / Science, March 2026 (cross-vendor study, 11 LLMs) Read the coverage ↗

14 points of accuracy lost — even after 20 hours of AI literacy training.

Single-blind RCT, 44 physicians, all of whom completed a 20-hour AI literacy program. When LLM suggestions contained errors, diagnostic accuracy dropped 14 percentage points (73.3% vs 84.9% control). The lesson cuts deep: institutional training, by itself, doesn't prevent automation bias. The user-side intervention isn't optional — it's the missing layer.

Qazi et al., 2026 — NEJM AI (RCT) Read the paper ↗

Your mindset shapes how AI shapes you

A 200-student SEM study found that critical thinking and attitude toward AI determine whether students engage reflectively or passively — and that passive adoption patterns are linked to cognitive offloading risk. The tool is neutral. The posture isn't.

Ninghardjanti et al., 2025 — Frontiers in Education Read the paper ↗

Harvard faculty are asking the question out loud

"No learning occurs unless the brain is actively engaged in making meaning." Harvard researchers and faculty are raising the alarm: passive AI use may be producing cognitive atrophy. Not a fringe concern — mainstream academic conversation, November 2025.
