The Fluency Trap — why passive acceptance happens. Automation bias gradually extinguishes monitoring behavior as AI outputs become more fluent.

Passive AI use showed a 75% correlation with decline in critical thinking

A peer-reviewed study of 666 participants found that the more people offloaded thinking to AI, the measurably worse their independent critical thinking became.

Gerlich, 2025 — Societies (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

All of this research is correlational — measured after the fact.

No one has tested whether real-time cognitive awareness feedback changes how people engage with AI while it's happening.

We want to run that study. Here's the design →

666 people. The data is unambiguous.

AI tool use correlated −0.68 with critical thinking scores. Cognitive offloading correlated −0.75. Two independent measures, one conclusion: the more you delegate to AI, the harder independent reasoning becomes. Significant at p < 0.001.

Gerlich, 2025 — Societies (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

It starts as convenience. It ends as dependency.

Kabashkin maps AI reliance as a 4-stage progression: tool → shortcut → bypass → full cognitive externalization. The risk isn't sudden — it's a slow drift "from comprehension to prompting, from synthesis to verification, from problem-solving to consumption." Reflective use reverses it. Passive use accelerates it.

Kabashkin, 2025 — Information (MDPI) Read the paper ↗

Your mindset shapes how AI shapes you

A 200-student SEM study found that critical thinking and attitude toward AI determine whether students engage reflectively or passively — and that passive adoption patterns are linked to cognitive offloading risk. The tool is neutral. The posture isn't.

Ninghardjanti et al., 2025 — Frontiers in Education Read the paper ↗
