## Evidence Synthesis — Hugonomy Thesis

### Evidence cluster A: Cognitive offloading and cognitive disengagement

A 2026 arXiv paper, “Why Johnny Can't Think: GenAI's Impacts on Cognitive Engagement,” reports a survey-model study of 299 STEM students across five North American universities and finds that trust-driven routine GenAI use was associated with significantly lower self-reported cognitive engagement, including reflection, need for understanding, and critical thinking habits. This supports Hugonomy’s intervention target: not blocking AI, but interrupting passive delegation.

A 2026 arXiv paper on critical thinking and AI use reports a mixed pattern: AI can support speed and learning, but dependence-related tendencies and reduced patience for sustained effort are associated with weaker reasoning performance. This maps well to Hugonomy’s distinction between “with AI” and “through AI.”

A 2026 arXiv paper on agile teams finds that AI-only planning minimized time/cost but degraded risk capture and increased rework from unstated assumptions, while hybrid planning preserved human judgment for ambiguity and risk. This is strong B2B language for Hugonomy: efficiency is not the same as effectiveness.

### Evidence cluster B: Institutional concern is increasing

Recent reporting from Australia describes employers warning that graduates are becoming overly reliant on AI, with gaps in communication, emotional intelligence, resilience, time management, professionalism, and real-world problem-solving. This is a market signal for Hugonomy’s workforce-readiness framing.

OECD-related reporting warns about AI creating “false mastery,” where fluent generated outputs mask weak underlying learning. This strongly supports Hugonomy’s “fluency trap” framing.

### Evidence cluster C: Counterbalance — AI can be used well

A Washington Post report describes teachers using ChatGPT as a writing partner, with students asked to critique, accept, reject, and reflect on AI feedback. This is the constructive side Hugonomy should emphasize: the product is not anti-AI; it is pro-metacognition.

## Anti-Bias / Red-Team Notes

Do not overclaim causality where studies are survey-based, exploratory, or preprint-stage. The strongest current claim is not “AI makes students dumb.” The defensible claim is: “Certain patterns of routine, high-trust AI use appear associated with lower cognitive engagement and may create over-reliance risks; tools should be designed to preserve reflection, verification, and human judgment.”

Avoid fear-first messaging. The market already hears too much AI panic. Hugonomy’s sharper posture is: “AI is powerful; therefore the interface must keep the human cognitively present.”
